
The purpose of this chapter is to show the fixed-structure versus
random-rotation difference established in the reduced
linear-attention theory of \citet{bordelon2026scaling}.

Chapters \ref{ch:theoremA} and \ref{ch:theoremB_stationary_circulant} have already
outlined that one should study the induced
{sample-space} operator rather than the raw feature-side matrix
appearing in the reduced-\(\Gamma\) model. Chapter \ref{ch:theoremB_stationary_circulant} treated the fixed-basis
stationary spectral regime, where exact circulant diagonalization yileds a fully
modewise closure.

The aim of this chapter is to study the
intermediate regime in which the spectral basis is no longer fixed across
contexts, but drifts randomly within bands. This change should produce a
symmetry-restricted effective operator class which is neither fully diagonal nor
fully scalar, but block-scalar in spectral coordinates. The main focus of this
chapter is therefore a symmetry theorem, a commutant theorem, an exact grouped
loss formula, and a rigorous refinement theorem explaining why basis-adaptive
hybrids use a fixed-depth advantage.

Before we begin with the proofs, we should first outline a major notation that will be used
in this chapter. Throughout this chapter we will work on the real vector space
\(\Herm(P)\subset \CC^{P\times P}\) of Hermitian matrices with the
Frobenius inner product
\[
\inner{A}{B}_F := \Re\Tr(A^*B).
\]
The main reasoning behind this choice is that this convention avoids the
irrelevant distinction between real and complex Fourier coordinates.
In particular, all exact formulas remain valid in the real
setting after replacing adjoints by transposes and restricting attention to the
natural conjugation-symmetric subclasses. We also use the normalization
\(P^{-1}\Tr\) for all population losses. Finally, modes with
\(\omega_i\lambda_i=0\) contribute identically zero to the loss therefore can
be removed from optimization statements. However in the cases that needed, we refer to the set of
active modes that we use as
\[
I_{\mathrm{act}}:=\{i\in [P]:\omega_i\lambda_i>0\}.
\]
\section{Band-Random-Rotation Setup}
We begin by defining the intermediate symmetry class studied in this chapter.
The point is to interpolate between a fixed spectral basis and full
random-rotation isotropy by allowing Haar rotations only within prescribed
bands.

We first specify how the spectral modes are grouped into bands.
\begin{definition}[Band partition]
\label{def:theoremC_band_partition}
Fix a partition of the spectral index set \([P]:=\{1,\dots,P\}\) into nonempty
blocks \(\cB=\{B_1,\dots,B_M\}\), \([P]=B_1\sqcup\cdots\sqcup B_M\), and \(m_b:=|B_b|\).
The numbers \(m_b\) are called the {band sizes}. Singleton bands
\((m_b=1)\) recover the fixed-basis spectral regime of Chapter \ref{ch:theoremB_stationary_circulant},
while one single band of size \(P\) recovers the full random-rotation isotropy regime.
\end{definition}

We next define the random ensemble generated by within-band basis drift.
\begin{definition}[Band-RRS ensemble]
\label{def:theoremC_band_rrs}
Let \(F_P\in\CC^{P\times P}\) be a fixed unitary spectral basis, and let
\(\Lambda=\diag(\lambda_1,\dots,\lambda_P)\succeq 0\) and
\(\Omega=\diag(\omega_1,\dots,\omega_P)\succeq 0\).
For each context draw, we let \( R=\bigoplus_{b=1}^M R_b\), and 
\(R_b\sim \mathrm{Haar}(O(m_b))\) be 
independent across bands. The corresponding matched band-random-rotation
ensemble is the family of operators we denote as 
\(T_R:=F_P^*R\Lambda R^*F_P\), and
\( \Omega_R:=F_P^*R\Omega R^*F_P\).
Thus the random within-band basis drift is encoded by blockwise Haar rotations
in spectral coordinates, while the diagonal spectra \(\Lambda\) and \(\Omega\)
remain fixed.
\end{definition}

We now state the population objective induced by this ensemble.
\begin{definition}[Population fixed-depth band-RRS loss]
\label{def:theoremC_population_loss}
For fixed depth \(L\ge 1\) and an effective sample-space operator
\(Q\in\Herm(P)\), we define
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:theoremC_population_loss}
\cE_L(Q)
:=
\frac{1}{P}\,
\EE_R\,\Tr\!\left[
\Omega_R
\Bigl(\Bigl(I-\frac{1}{L}QT_R\Bigr)^L\Bigr)^*
T_R
\Bigl(I-\frac{1}{L}QT_R\Bigr)^L
\right].
\end{equation}
This is the band-RRS population version of the stationary spectral loss we saw earlier in
Chapter \ref{ch:theoremB_stationary_circulant}, now averaged over random within-band basis drift.
\end{definition}

This also singles out the natural symmetry group and its fixed operator class.
\begin{definition}[Band-rotation group and commutant class]
\label{def:theoremC_commutant_class}
Define the band-rotation group as
\[
G_{\cB}
:=
F_P^*\left(\prod_{b=1}^M O(m_b)\right)F_P
\subset U(P),
\]
and its commutant class
\[
\cC_{\cB}
:=
\Comm(G_{\cB})
=
\{Q\in \Herm(P): UQU^*=Q\ \text{for all } U\in G_{\cB}\}.
\]
%The class \(\cC_{\cB}\) is the exact spectral-only model class compatible with
%the band-RRS symmetry.
\end{definition}
\section{Symmetry and Commutant Structure}
With the setup in place, we introduce the exact symmetry group of the
population loss and the operator class it preserves. This shows that bandwise
random rotations force the spectral-only dynamics to close on block-scalar
operators.

We first verify that the loss itself respects bandwise rotational symmetry.
\begin{lemma}[Band-rotation invariance of the population loss]
\label{lem:theoremC_loss_invariance}
For every fixed \(L\ge 1\), every \(Q\in\Herm(P)\), and every
\(U\in G_{\cB}\), one has
\( \cE_L(UQU^*)=\cE_L(Q)\).
\end{lemma}

\end{proof}

With that in place, we identify all operators compatible with that symmetry.
\begin{proposition}[Characterization of the band commutant]
\label{prop:theoremC_commutant_characterization}
The commutant class is exactly
\[
\cC_{\cB}
=
\left\{
F_P^*\left(\bigoplus_{b=1}^M q_b I_{m_b}\right)F_P:
q_b\in\RR
\right\}.
\]
\end{proposition}

\end{proof}
This symmetry class is also dynamically stable under population gradient flow.
\begin{proposition}[Gradient-flow preservation of the band commutant]
\label{prop:theoremC_gradient_flow_commutant}
Let \(Q(t)\) solve the population gradient-flow equation
\(
\dot Q(t)=-\nabla_Q \cE_L(Q(t))
\)
on \(\Herm(P)\). If \(Q(0)\in\cC_{\cB}\), then
\(Q(t)\in\cC_{\cB}\) for every time at which the solution exists. In
particular, from \(Q(0)=0\) there exist scalar block coordinates
\(q_1(t),\dots,q_M(t)\) such that
\[
Q(t)=F_P^*\left(\bigoplus_{b=1}^M q_b(t)I_{m_b}\right)F_P.
\]
\end{proposition}

\section{Exact Grouped Closure and Reduced Dynamics}
So far, we identified the invariant commutant. We can now reduce the full population
dynamics to a finite-dimensional grouped system. The result is an exact
blockwise loss formula together with a gradient-flow ODE for the band
coordinates.

We can now collapse the full operator dynamics to grouped scalar coordinates.
\begin{theorem}[Exact grouped closure under band-RRS]
\label{thm:theoremC_grouped_closure}
Assume the matched band-RRS ensemble of
Definitions~\ref{def:theoremC_band_partition}--\ref{def:theoremC_commutant_class},
and let \(Q(t)\) evolve by population gradient flow from \(Q(0)=0\). Then there
exist scalar block variables \(q_1(t),\dots,q_M(t)\) such that
\[
Q(t)=F_P^*\left(\bigoplus_{b=1}^M q_b(t)I_{m_b}\right)F_P.
\]
Writing \(q=(q_1,\dots,q_M)\), the population loss reduces exactly to
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:theoremC_grouped_loss}
\cE_L(q)
=
\frac{1}{P}
\sum_{b=1}^M\sum_{i\in B_b}
\omega_i\lambda_i
\left(1-\frac{\lambda_i q_b}{L}\right)^{2L}.
\end{equation}
Moreover, if one identifies the commutant class with \(\RR^M\) through the map
\[
\Phi(q):=F_P^*\left(\bigoplus_{b=1}^M q_b I_{m_b}\right)F_P,
\]
then the induced metric is
\(
\norm{\Phi(q)}_F^2 = \sum_{b=1}^M m_b q_b^2,
\)
and the gradient-flow dynamics on the block coordinates satisfy
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:theoremC_grouped_ode}
m_b\,\dot q_b(t)
=
-\partial_{q_b}\cE_L(q(t))
=
\frac{2}{P}
\sum_{i\in B_b}
\omega_i\lambda_i^2
\left(1-\frac{\lambda_i q_b(t)}{L}\right)^{2L-1}.
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By Proposition~\ref{prop:theoremC_gradient_flow_commutant}, the entire gradient
flow trajectory remains in the commutant, so there exist real scalars
\(q_b(t)\) such that
\(
Q(t)=\Phi(q(t))=F_P^* D_q F_P\) where
\(
D_q:=\bigoplus_{b=1}^M q_b I_{m_b}.
\)
We simply need to compute the exact loss and the induced ODE in these coordinates.

We first show the exact reduction of the loss as in \eqref{eq:theoremC_grouped_loss}.
Substitute \(Q=F_P^* D_q F_P\) into
\eqref{eq:theoremC_population_loss}. By unitary invariance of the trace,
\begin{align}
\cE_L(q)
&=
\frac{1}{P}\,
\EE_R\,
\Tr\!\left[
R\Omega R^*
\Bigl(\Bigl(I-\frac{1}{L}D_q R\Lambda R^*\Bigr)^L\Bigr)^*
R\Lambda R^*
\Bigl(I-\frac{1}{L}D_q R\Lambda R^*\Bigr)^L
\right].
\label{eq:theoremC_loss_spectral_basis}
\end{align}
Now \(D_q\) is block-scalar with the same band decomposition as \(R\), hence
\(D_qR=RD_q\). Therefore
\[
R\Bigl(I-\frac{1}{L}D_q\Lambda\Bigr)R^*
=
I-\frac{1}{L}RD_q\Lambda R^*
=
I-\frac{1}{L}D_q R\Lambda R^*.
\]
Raising to the \(L\)-th power gives
\[
\Bigl(I-\frac{1}{L}D_qR\Lambda R^*\Bigr)^L
=
R\Bigl(I-\frac{1}{L}D_q\Lambda\Bigr)^L R^*,
\]
and similarly for the adjoint. Substituting into
\eqref{eq:theoremC_loss_spectral_basis}, all occurrences of \(R\) cancel by
cyclicity of the trace such that
\begin{align*}
\cE_L(q)
&=
\frac{1}{P}\,
\Tr\!\left[
\Omega
\Bigl(\Bigl(I-\frac{1}{L}D_q\Lambda\Bigr)^L\Bigr)^*
\Lambda
\Bigl(I-\frac{1}{L}D_q\Lambda\Bigr)^L
\right].
\end{align*}
Since \(D_q\), \(\Lambda\), and \(\Omega\) are diagonal in the same basis and all
relevant scalars are real, the matrix inside the trace is diagonal with
entries
\(
\omega_i\lambda_i(1-\lambda_i q_{b(i)}/L)^{2L}
\), where \(b(i)\) denotes the
unique band containing \(i\). Summing over bands yields
\eqref{eq:theoremC_grouped_loss}.

We next compute the induced metric on the commutant coordinates.
For \(q,q'\in\RR^M\),
\begin{align*}
\inner{\Phi(q)}{\Phi(q')}_F
&=
\Re\Tr\!\left[
F_P^*D_qF_P F_P^*D_{q'}F_P
\right]
=
\Re\Tr(D_qD_{q'}) \\
&=
\sum_{b=1}^M m_b q_b q_b'.
\end{align*}
Hence
\(
\norm{\Phi(q)}_F^2=\sum_b m_b q_b^2
\).

Finally, derive the grouped gradient-flow system.
Let \(E_b\in\CC^{P\times P}\) be the diagonal matrix which is the identity on
block \(B_b\) and zero elsewhere, and define
\(
\Phi_b := F_P^* E_b F_P = \Phi(e_b),
\)
where \(e_b\) is the \(b\)-th standard basis vector in \(\RR^M\). Then
\(
\inner{\Phi_b}{\Phi_c}_F = m_b\delta_{bc}
\). Differentiating
\(Q(t)=\sum_b q_b(t)\Phi_b\) gives
\(
\dot Q(t)=\sum_{b=1}^M \dot q_b(t)\Phi_b.
\)

Because \(\dot Q(t)=-\nabla_Q\cE_L(Q(t))\), taking the Frobenius inner product
with \(\Phi_b\) yields
\[
\sum_{c=1}^M \dot q_c(t)\inner{\Phi_c}{\Phi_b}_F
=
-\inner{\nabla_Q\cE_L(Q(t))}{\Phi_b}_F.
\]
The left-hand side simplifies to \(m_b\dot q_b(t)\). The right-hand side is
exactly \(-\partial_{q_b}\cE_L(q(t))\) by the chain rule. Therefore
\(
m_b\dot q_b(t) = -\partial_{q_b}\cE_L(q(t)).
\)

Therefore, the only thing we need is to compute the derivative of
\eqref{eq:theoremC_grouped_loss}. Since only the terms with indices
\(i\in B_b\) depend on \(q_b\),
\begin{align*}
\partial_{q_b}\cE_L(q)
&=
\frac{1}{P}
\sum_{i\in B_b}
\omega_i\lambda_i\cdot 2L
\left(1-\frac{\lambda_i q_b}{L}\right)^{2L-1}
\left(-\frac{\lambda_i}{L}\right) \\
&=
-\frac{2}{P}
\sum_{i\in B_b}
\omega_i\lambda_i^2
\left(1-\frac{\lambda_i q_b}{L}\right)^{2L-1}.
\end{align*}
Substituting this into the metric gradient-flow equation gives
\eqref{eq:theoremC_grouped_ode}. This completes the proof.
\end{proof}

This exact closure recovers the two endpoint regimes as special cases.
\begin{corollary}[Recovery of the two endpoints]
\label{cor:theoremC_endpoints}
Theorem~\ref{thm:theoremC_grouped_closure} recovers the two limiting regimes as follows
\begin{enumerate}
    \item If every band is a singleton, then
    \[
    Q(t)=F_P^*\diag(q_1(t),\dots,q_P(t))F_P,
    \]
    and one recovers the modewise closure of the fixed-basis spectral regime.
    \item If there is a single band of size \(P\), then \(Q(t)=q(t)I\) and the
    dynamics reduce to a single scalar isotropic variable,
    %similar to the full random-rotation isotropy mechanism of \citet{bordelon2026scaling}.
\end{enumerate}
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}[Proof deferred to Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_theoremC_proofs}]
~
\end{proof}
\section{Finite-Depth Spectral-Only Limits}
This section shows the optimization consequences of the grouped reduction at
finite depth. In particular, it characterizes the best spectral-only risk,
identifies when zero block error is possible, and quantifies the obstruction
created by within-band heterogeneity.

The grouped formula immediately yields the best achievable spectral-only loss at fixed depth.
\begin{proposition}[Optimal spectral-only fixed-depth risk]
\label{prop:theoremC_optimal_spectral_only_risk}
Define the optimal band-commutant loss by
\(
\cE_{L,\cB}^{\star}
:=
\inf_{Q\in \cC_{\cB}} \cE_L(Q)
\).
Then
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:theoremC_optimal_block_loss}
\cE_{L,\cB}^{\star}
=
\frac{1}{P}
\sum_{b=1}^M
\inf_{q_b\in\RR}
\sum_{i\in B_b}
\omega_i\lambda_i
\left(1-\frac{\lambda_i q_b}{L}\right)^{2L}.
\end{equation}
Moreover, every spectral-only gradient-flow trajectory from \(Q(0)=0\) has
\(
\cE_L(Q(t))\ge \cE_{L,\cB}^{\star}
\)
for all
\(
t\ge 0.
\)
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Proof deferred to Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_theoremC_proofs}]
~
\end{proof}

At depth one, the blockwise optimization problem becomes explicitly solvable.
\begin{corollary}[Closed-form block optimum at depth \texorpdfstring{$L=1$}{L=1}]
\label{cor:theoremC_L1_block_optimum}
Fix a block \(B_b\) and assume it is \emph{active}, i.e.
\(
\sum_{i\in B_b}\omega_i\lambda_i^3>0
\).
At depth \(L=1\), the block objective is
\[
\mathcal E_b(q_b)
:=
\sum_{i\in B_b}\omega_i\lambda_i(1-q_b\lambda_i)^2.
\]
Its unique minimizer is
\[
q_b^{\star}
=
\frac{\sum_{i\in B_b}\omega_i\lambda_i^2}
{\sum_{i\in B_b}\omega_i\lambda_i^3},
\]
and the corresponding minimal block loss is
\[
\mathcal E_b^{\star}
=
\sum_{i\in B_b}\omega_i\lambda_i
-
\frac{\left(\sum_{i\in B_b}\omega_i\lambda_i^2\right)^2}
{\sum_{i\in B_b}\omega_i\lambda_i^3}.
\]
If a block is inactive, then \(\mathcal E_b(q_b)\equiv 0\) and every
\(q_b\in\RR\) is optimal. Consequently,
\(
\cE^{\star}_{1,\cB}
=
\frac{1}{P}\sum_{b=1}^M \mathcal E_b^{\star},
\)
where the convention for inactive blocks is \(\mathcal E_b^{\star}=0\).
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}[Proof deferred to Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_theoremC_proofs}]
~
\end{proof}

The next criterion isolates exactly when a single block can be fit with zero finite-depth error.
\begin{corollary}[Finite-depth heterogeneity criterion]
\label{cor:theoremC_heterogeneity_obstruction}
Fix a block \(B_b\) and a finite depth \(L\ge 1\). Then
\[
\inf_{q_b\in\RR}
\sum_{i\in B_b}
\omega_i\lambda_i
\left(1-\frac{\lambda_i q_b}{L}\right)^{2L}
=0
\]
if and only if the eigenvalues are constant on the active support of the block,
meaning \(\lambda_i=\lambda_j\) for all \(i,j \in B_b\) such that
\(\omega_i\lambda_i>0\) and \(\omega_j\lambda_j>0\).

\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}[Proof deferred to Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_theoremC_proofs}]
~
\end{proof}

A simple choice of block parameter also gives an explicit finite-depth decay bound.
\begin{corollary}[Condition-number phase diagnostic]
\label{cor:theoremC_condition_number}
Assume block \(B_b\) is active and define minimum
\(
\lambda_{\min}^{(b)}:=\min_{i\in B_b\cap I_{\mathrm{act}}}\lambda_i,
\)
and maximum
\(
\lambda_{\max}^{(b)}:=\max_{i\in B_b\cap I_{\mathrm{act}}}\lambda_i,
\)
where
\(
\kappa_b:=\frac{\lambda_{\max}^{(b)}}{\lambda_{\min}^{(b)}}
\)
Set
\(
\eta_b^{\sharp}:=\frac{2}{\lambda_{\max}^{(b)}+\lambda_{\min}^{(b)}},
\)
where
\(
q_b^{\sharp}(L):=L\eta_b^{\sharp}.
\)
Then
\[
\rho_b^{\sharp}
:=
\max_{i\in B_b\cap I_{\mathrm{act}}}
\left|1-\eta_b^{\sharp}\lambda_i\right|
=
\frac{\kappa_b-1}{\kappa_b+1},
\]
and the block error is bounded by
\[
\sum_{i\in B_b}
\omega_i\lambda_i
\left(1-\frac{\lambda_i q_b^{\sharp}(L)}{L}\right)^{2L}
\le
\left(\sum_{i\in B_b}\omega_i\lambda_i\right)
(\rho_b^{\sharp})^{2L}.
\]
\end{corollary}

~
\end{proof}
\section{Refinement and Hybrid Advantage}
In this final section of the chapter we compare coarse and refined band partitions to formalize how extra
spectral resolution enlarges the admissible operator class. This yields a
monotonicity argument and then a strict-gain criterion explaining when
basis-adaptive hybrids can outperform coarse spectral-only models.
To compare coarse and fine spectral structure, we now formalize refinement of partitions.
\begin{definition}[Refinement of band partitions]
\label{def:theoremC_refinement}
Let \(\cB' = \{B'_1,\dots,B'_{M'}\}\) be another partition of \([P]\). We write
\(
\cB' \preceq \cB
\)
if every refined block \(B'_\alpha\) is contained in some coarse block
\(B_b\). In that case define the refined commutant class
\[
\cC_{\cB'}
:=
\left\{
F_P^*\left(\bigoplus_{\alpha=1}^{M'} q'_\alpha I_{|B'_\alpha|}\right)F_P:
q'_\alpha\in\RR
\right\}.
\]
Thus a refinement corresponds exactly to enlarging the admissible block-scalar
operator class in spectral coordinates.
\end{definition}

Now we show that refining the partition enlarges the admissible commutant and can only improve the optimum.
\begin{proposition}[Monotonicity under refinement]
\label{prop:theoremC_refinement_monotonicity}
If \(\cB'\preceq \cB\), then
\(
\cC_{\cB}\subseteq \cC_{\cB'}
\) 
and hence
\(
\cE^{\star}_{L,\cB'}\le \cE^{\star}_{L,\cB}.
\)
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Proof deferred to Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_theoremC_proofs}]
~
\end{proof}
We now show that there is a strict gain once a coarse block mixes subblocks with genuinely different preferred scalars.
\begin{corollary}[Strict hybrid gain under heterogeneous coarse blocks]
\label{cor:theoremC_strict_hybrid_gain}
Assume \(\cB'\preceq \cB\). Suppose there exists a coarse block
\(B_b\in \cB\) which is split by the refinement into subblocks
\(C_1,\dots,C_s\) such that the corresponding one-parameter minimizers of the
subblock objectives
\[
\phi_u(q):=\sum_{i\in C_u}\omega_i\lambda_i
\left(1-\frac{\lambda_i q}{L}\right)^{2L}
\qquad (u=1,\dots,s)
\]
are not all identical. Then
\(
\cE^{\star}_{L,\cB'} < \cE^{\star}_{L,\cB}.
\)
In particular, if a positive-weight coarse block contains two distinct active
eigenvalues and the refinement separates those modes into singleton blocks, then
the inequality is strict.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}[Proof deferred to Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_theoremC_proofs}]
~
\end{proof}
\section{Conditional Hybrid Bounds and Scaling}
The preceding sections were exact and symmetry based. We now isolate the extra
quantitative assumptions needed to convert the refinement advantage into an
architecture-level hybrid bound. Under projector-estimation and
spectral-approximation control, this yields a conditional win criterion and a
separable mixture-scaling law.
We now introduce an abstract hybrid framework that separates basis adaptation from spectral fitting.
\begin{definition}[Abstract hybrid loss and projector families]
\label{def:theoremC_hybrid_loss}
Fix a refinement \(\cB'\preceq \cB\). For each context draw, let
\(\Pi=(\Pi_\alpha)_\alpha\) denote the true projector family onto the refined
subspaces, and let \(\widehat\Pi=(\widehat\Pi_\alpha)_\alpha\) denote a family of
estimated projectors produced by a basis-adaptive module. Let
\(\cE_{L_S}(\Pi,Q)\) denote the corresponding hybrid population loss obtained by
transporting a spectral backbone operator \(Q\in \cC_{\cB'}\) through the
projector family \(\Pi\). The exact architecture-level realization of
\(\cE_{L_S}(\Pi,Q)\) is intentionally left abstract. The only properties used
below are the stability and backbone-approximation hypotheses stated next.
\end{definition}

The first extra hypothesis controls how projector estimation errors perturb the loss.
\begin{assumption}[Projector perturbation stability]
\label{ass:theoremC_projector_stability}
There exists a constant \(C_{\mathrm{proj}}>0\) such that for every admissible
refined-basis operator \(Q\in \cC_{\cB'}\),
\[
\bigl|\cE_{L_S}(\widehat\Pi,Q)-\cE_{L_S}(\Pi,Q)\bigr|
\le
C_{\mathrm{proj}}\sum_{\alpha}\EE\,\norm{\widehat\Pi_\alpha-\Pi_\alpha}_{\op}.
\]
\end{assumption}

The second hypothesis quantifies the accuracy achieved by the adaptive basis module.
\begin{assumption}[Basis-adaptation rate]
\label{ass:theoremC_basis_adaptation_rate}
The basis-adaptive module with context length \(P\) and adaptive depth \(L_A\)
produces estimated projectors satisfying
\[
\sum_{\alpha}\EE\,\norm{\widehat\Pi_\alpha-\Pi_\alpha}_{\op}
\le
\varepsilon_A(P,L_A).
\]
\end{assumption}

Under these assumptions, the hybrid excess loss splits into basis and spectral contributions.
\begin{proposition}[Conditional hybrid upper bound]
\label{prop:theoremC_conditional_hybrid_upper_bound}
Assume Definitions~\ref{def:theoremC_refinement} and
\ref{def:theoremC_hybrid_loss}, together with
Assumptions~\ref{ass:theoremC_projector_stability} and
\ref{ass:theoremC_basis_adaptation_rate}. Suppose further that the spectral
backbone, when evaluated in the \emph{true} refined basis, produces some
operator \(\widehat Q\in \cC_{\cB'}\) satisfying
\[
\cE_{L_S}(\Pi,\widehat Q)-\cE^{\star}_{L_S,\cB'}
\le
C_S\,\mathcal E_{\mathrm{spec}}(L_S,r),
\]
where \(L_S\) is the spectral depth and \(r\) is the spectral bottleneck
parameter. Then the hybrid predictor satisfies
\[
\cE^{\mathrm{hyb}}-
\cE^{\star}_{L_S,\cB'}
\le
C_A\,\varepsilon_A(P,L_A)
+
C_S\,\mathcal E_{\mathrm{spec}}(L_S,r),
\]
where \(C_A:=C_{\mathrm{proj}}\) and
\(\cE^{\mathrm{hyb}}:=\cE_{L_S}(\widehat\Pi,\widehat Q)\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Proof deferred to Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_theoremC_proofs}]

This immediately gives a sufficient condition for a strict hybrid advantage at finite depth.
\begin{corollary}[Finite-depth hybrid win criterion]
\label{cor:theoremC_finite_depth_hybrid_win}
Assume the hypotheses of
Proposition~\ref{prop:theoremC_conditional_hybrid_upper_bound}. If
\[
C_A\,\varepsilon_A(P,L_A)
+
C_S\,\mathcal E_{\mathrm{spec}}(L_S,r)
<
\cE^{\star}_{L,\cB}-\cE^{\star}_{L,\cB'},
\]
then the corresponding hybrid architecture strictly outperforms every
spectral-only trajectory constrained to the coarse commutant class
\(\cC_{\cB}\) at that fixed finite depth.
\end{corollary}

Adding standard scaling hypotheses then yields a separable excess-risk law.
\begin{proposition}[Conditional hybrid mixture-scaling law]
\label{prop:theoremC_conditional_mixture_law}
Assume the hypotheses of
Proposition~\ref{prop:theoremC_conditional_hybrid_upper_bound}. In addition,
assume:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item the spectral-side asymptotics
    \[
    \mathcal E_{\mathrm{spec}}(L_S,r)
    \lesssim
    c_S L_S^{-\beta_S}+c_r r^{-\beta_r};
    \]
    \item the basis-adaptation rate
    \[
    \varepsilon_A(P,L_A)
    \lesssim
    c_A' L_A^{-\beta_A}+c_P P^{-\beta_P};
    \]
    \item an optimization-time term
    \[
    \mathcal E_t(t)\lesssim c_t t^{-\beta_t};
    \]
    \item an additive excess-risk decomposition of the form
    \[
    \cE(t,L_S,L_A,r,P)-\cE^\star
    \le
    \mathcal E_t(t)
    +
    \bigl(\cE^{\mathrm{hyb}}-\cE^{\star}_{L_S,\cB'}\bigr).
    \]
\end{enumerate}
Then the total hybrid excess loss obeys the separable estimate
\[
\cE(t,L_S,L_A,r,P)-\cE^\star
\lesssim
c_t t^{-\beta_t}
+
c_P P^{-\beta_P}
+
c_r r^{-\beta_r}
+
c_S L_S^{-\beta_S}
+
c_A L_A^{-\beta_A},
\]
for a constant \(c_A\) depending on \(C_A\) and \(c_A'\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{remark}[Logical role of the exact and conditional parts]
\label{rem:theoremC_exact_vs_conditional}
The exact core of Theorem~C consists of
Lemma~\ref{lem:theoremC_loss_invariance},
Propositions~\ref{prop:theoremC_commutant_characterization},
\ref{prop:theoremC_gradient_flow_commutant},

estimation and spectral approximation rates which lies beyond the purely
symmetry-based core of the theorem.
\end{remark}
